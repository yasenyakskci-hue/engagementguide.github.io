Hoş geldiniz — Welcome

Thank you for travelling to celebrate with us.

We chose Adana because it's Yasenya's home — the city where she was born and grew up. It's a warm, generous place on the Seyhan River in southern Türkiye, famous for its food, its hospitality, and long evenings outdoors. Many of you are travelling far to be here, so we've put together everything you need: how to arrive, where to stay, what to eat, what to see, and the plan for the big day.

Adana isn't a polished tourist town — that's the charm. It's a real city that rewards the curious: smoky kebab houses that have run for generations, a Roman bridge still in use, one of Türkiye's largest mosques mirrored in the river, and citrus groves on the plain beyond. Come hungry.

Getting There & Around

Arriving in Adana

Adana is well connected to Istanbul, Ankara and several European cities. Here's how to get into the city and move around once you're here.

✈

Fly into Çukurova Airport (COV) — not the old one

Since August 2024, all scheduled flights use the new Çukurova International Airport (COV) near Tarsus, about 40–45 minutes from central Adana. The old city-centre airport (Şakirpaşa / ADA) is closed to commercial flights — double-check your booking says COV.

1

Airport → city

Easiest is a taxi or Uber — order online in the app and pay by card (~40–45 min to the centre).

Most affordable is the Havaş shuttle bus. Its Optimum AVM stop is within walking distance if you're staying at HiltonSA, Sheraton or Bosnalı Hotel.

2

Buses & dolmuş

City buses reach all over Adana — and you can simply tap your credit or debit card to pay as you board. No travel card needed.

The blue dolmuş (shared minibuses) run fixed routes and also take card — just say "inecek var!" when you want to get off.

3

Taxis & walking

Taxis are everywhere and cheap. Use BiTaksi or Uber to book one easily and skip the haggling. Having your destination written down helps — English isn't widely spoken.

The centre, river, mosque and Central Park are all very walkable and best explored on foot.

Eat & Drink

This is why people come to Adana

Adana is one of Türkiye's great food cities. Eating here is the main event — generous, smoky, and built around the grill. Don't leave without trying these.

What to order

Adana kebabı — the spicy hand-minced lamb kebab, the city's namesake Ciğer — charcoal-grilled liver, often as a wrap (dürüm) Kaburga — succulent grilled ribs Şalgam — tangy fermented turnip juice (mild or spicy) İçli köfte — stuffed bulgur croquettes Bici bici — a rose-pink iced summer dessert Şırdan — for the adventurous Turkish breakfast — a long, sprawling weekend ritual
For kebab

Eski Dostlar or Yeşil Kapı

Our go-to spots for proper Adana kebabı. Eski Dostlar is in Ziyapaşa; Yeşil Kapı is a local favourite too. You can't go wrong with either.

For ciğer

Büyüksaat

For charcoal-grilled liver, head to the Büyüksaat (Great Clock Tower) area in the old town — this is the place for ciğer.

For şırdan

Şırdancı Bedo

Feeling adventurous? Şırdancı Bedo serves the best şırdan in town — a true Adana specialty for the curious eater.

Coffee & bars

Ziyapaşa, Toros & Bahar Caddesi

For coffee by day and drinks by night, wander Ziyapaşa, Gazipaşa Toros Caddesi or Bahar Caddesi — all lined with cafés, wine bars and lively spots. Anywhere here is a good bet.

A proper Turkish night out

Meyhane · Usul Meyhane

For the full rakı-and-meze experience with music and long tables, a meyhane is the way — and Usul Meyhane is a favourite of ours. If you'd like to join a pre-engagement dinner there, text Yasenya so she can reserve a place for all of us. Believe us — this is going to be a real Turkish experience.

See & Do

If you have time to explore

Whether you have a spare afternoon or a couple of extra days, Adana and the surrounding Çukurova plain reward a wander.

Landmark

Sabancı Central Mosque

One of Türkiye's largest mosques, with six minarets reflected in the Seyhan. Stunning at night. Dress modestly; women cover their hair.

Roman

Taşköprü (Stone Bridge)

A Roman-era bridge that still spans the Seyhan after nearly two millennia — lovely for a walk and photos, especially at sunset.

Green space

Merkez (Central) Park

Türkiye's largest city park, stretching along the river beneath the mosque. Walk, rent a bike, or take a little boat on the Seyhan.

Old town

Great Clock Tower & bazaar

The 1882 clock tower marks the heart of historic Adana — a warren of shops, coppersmiths, sweets and old kebapçıs.

History

Ulu Camii & Archaeology Museum

The 16th-century Grand Mosque with its distinctive stonework, plus a museum tracing the region's deep Roman and Hittite past.

Heritage

Tepebağ historic houses

Adana's oldest quarter, with traditional timber-and-stone rowhouses — atmospheric lanes that feel a world away from the modern city.

~1 hr · cinematic

Varda Bridge

A dramatic 100-year-old railway viaduct near Karaisalı — film fans may recognise it from a famous opening chase. Reachable by car or train.

~1 hr · nature

Kapıkaya Canyon

A scenic gorge with a hiking trail carved by the Çakıt River through the Taurus foothills — cool relief from the heat.

~30 min · castle

Yılankale (Snake Castle)

A 12th-century hilltop fortress with panoramic views over the Çukurova plain and the river. A short, rewarding climb.

~30 min · ancient

Misis (Yakapınar)

An ancient settlement with Roman mosaics and a bridge over the Ceyhan — easy to pair with a half-day out of the city.

~1 hr · liver kebab

Kozan

A hilltop castle, old houses, and — fittingly — some of the region's most famous grilled liver. Great for food pilgrims.

Seaside

Yumurtalık & the coast

Small Mediterranean towns with castles and beaches for a relaxed day by the sea when the city heat builds.

Practical Tips

The little things

Money

The currency is the Turkish Lira (₺). Cards work in hotels and most restaurants, but carry cash for taxis, the dolmuş and small kebab houses. ATMs are easy to find. Tipping ~5–10% is appreciated.

Weather

Adana is warm to very hot, especially May–September when it can climb well above 35°C with humidity. Pack light, breathable clothing, sunglasses, sunscreen and a refillable water bottle.

What to wear

For the engagement itself, go chic and elegant — wedding-level dressy. Around the city, keep it casual and light for the heat. No need to pack a scarf; if you do visit a mosque, head coverings are provided at the entrance.

Staying connected

A local SIM or eSIM gives cheap data; Wi-Fi is common in hotels and cafés. Download an offline map and a translation app — English isn't widely spoken outside hotels.
